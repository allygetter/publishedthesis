This study addresses a critical gap in East African pavement engineering: the performance of asphalt concrete modified with locally-sourced sisal fibre and waste tyre crumb rubber. Abstract

Road infrastructure is a fundamental enabler of socio-economic development in Kenya. This study assesses the performance of asphalt concrete modified with combined sisal fibre and crumb rubber at dosage levels of 0%, 1%, 2%, and 3% by weight of bitumen using the Marshall Method (ASTM D6927). Results demonstrate a 26.1% improvement in Marshall Stability at 3% modification (8.57 kN → 10.81 kN), a 28% peak improvement in Indirect Tensile Strength at 2% (820 → 1,050 kPa), and bitumen retention reaching 97% at 3% dosage. The optimum modifier content is identified as 2%–3%, offering a compelling case for adoption of these locally-sourced, waste-derived materials in sustainable road construction across Kenya.

1. Introduction

Road infrastructure is a fundamental enabler of socio-economic development, enhancing connectivity, trade, and access to essential services. Kenya's road network has expanded from approximately 41,800 km in 1963 to over 63,575 km by 2019 (KeNHA, 2019). Despite this growth, an estimated 30% of the total network requires major rehabilitation or complete reconstruction — largely attributed to structural failures arising from poor material performance, inadequate design specifications, and climatic stressors.

Conventional asphalt concrete often lacks sufficient tensile strength and structural stability to endure harsh service conditions, leading to frequent distress modes including rutting, cracking, stripping, and potholing. Simultaneously, Kenya faces a mounting environmental challenge from the improper disposal of waste vehicle tyres, which accumulate in landfills or are openly burned, posing serious threats to public health and ecological systems (Huaxin et al., 2009).

Sisal fibre (Agave sisalana) abundantly cultivated in Kitui, Taita-Taveta, and Kilifi counties possesses tensile strength of 300–700 MPa, thermal stability, and rough surface texture that promotes mechanical interlocking within composite matrices (Ramakrishna & Sundararajan, 2005). Crumb rubber (CR) from waste tyres improves viscoelastic behaviour of asphalt binders, enhancing elasticity, fatigue resistance, and high-temperature performance (Mashaan et al., 2014). While prior studies have examined each modifier individually, their synergistic application under Kenyan climatic and traffic conditions has received no attention. This study fills that gap using entirely locally-sourced materials.

◉ Research Gap

While prior studies have examined sisal fibre and crumb rubber individually as asphalt modifiers, the synergistic application of both materials in a single mix — particularly under Kenyan climatic and traffic conditions — has received no attention. This study fills that gap with empirical evidence using entirely locally-sourced materials.

Research Objectives

  1. Determine the Indirect Tensile Strength (ITS) at 0%, 1%, 2%, and 3% combined sisal fibre and crumb rubber by weight of bitumen.
  2. Determine Marshall Stability and Flow at the same dosage levels.
  3. Assess binder–aggregate adhesion through bitumen affinity (stripping) tests across all four modifier contents.

2. Methodology

2.1 Materials

All materials were sourced locally to ensure applicability to Kenyan road construction. Aggregates were obtained from Mlolongo Quarry in the Nairobi Metropolitan area (crushed basalt/granite). Bitumen used was 80/100 penetration grade from Kenya Petroleum Refineries Ltd. Sisal Fibre was sourced from Kitui County and trimmed to 10–15 mm uniform length. Crumb Rubber was obtained from waste tyres at Dandora Dumpsite and ground to pass a 1.18 mm sieve.

2.2 Mix Design

The Marshall Method (ASTM D6927) was followed for Asphalt Concrete Type I (AC-I) targeting: Stability ≥ 8 kN, Flow 2–4 mm, Air Voids 3–5%, VMA ≥ 14%, and VFB 65–75%. Trial mixes at bitumen contents of 4.5%–6.5% established the Optimum Bitumen Binder Content (OBBC) at 5.5%. Modified mixes incorporated equal proportions of sisal fibre and crumb rubber as dry additives at 1%, 2%, and 3% by weight of bitumen; mixing temperature was maintained at 150–160°C with 75 blows per side compaction. Two replicate specimens were prepared per mix category.

3. Results

3.1 Marshall Stability and Flow

Marshall Stability increased progressively from 8.57 kN (control) to 10.81 kN at 3% modification — a 26.1% improvement. The improved interlocking between binder and sisal fibres and elastic recovery from crumb rubber explain this trend. Bulk density decreased slightly from 2.21 to 2.15 g/cm³, reflecting modestly increased internal voids. Flow values rose from 9.0 to 11.0 mm, indicating enhanced ductility and energy absorption — desirable for fatigue cracking resistance.

Key Insight

All modified mixes exceeded the 8.0 kN minimum specification. The increased flow values reflect rubber-enhanced plasticity — a known characteristic of CR-modified asphalt that warrants re-evaluation of conventional flow specifications for rubber-modified mixtures.

3.2 Indirect Tensile Strength (ITS)

ITS followed a non-monotonic trend: rising from 820 kPa (control) to a peak of 1,050 kPa at 2% before a marginal decline to 1,010 kPa at 3%. The 28% improvement at 2% reflects synergistic crack-bridging of sisal fibres combined with elastic energy absorption of crumb rubber. The slight decrease at 3% is attributed to fibre agglomeration and reduced homogeneity at higher dosages — a phenomenon also observed by Gungor & Topcu (2020).

The 2% modifier content represents the inflection point for tensile strength, achieving a 28% improvement over the control. Beyond this threshold, diminishing returns due to fibre agglomeration begin to offset reinforcement benefits. Practical recommendations should target the 2%–3% window.

3.3 Bitumen Affinity (Stripping) Test

Retained coating percentage increased consistently with modifier content, confirming improved binder–aggregate adhesion and enhanced resistance to moisture-induced damage. The 3% mix exhibited the best performance at 97% retained coating — a strong indicator of moisture-damage resistance consistent with findings by Little & Bhasin (2006).

4. Conclusions & Recommendations

This study demonstrates that combined sisal fibre and crumb rubber modification of asphalt concrete yields measurable performance improvements across all tested parameters. The 2%–3% modifier content range is identified as optimal, balancing tensile strength (28% peak gain at 2%) with stability (26.1% gain at 3%) and moisture resistance (97% bitumen retention at 3%). All modified mixes exceeded the minimum 8.0 kN Marshall Stability specification.

Future research should: (i) conduct full-scale field trials under real traffic and climatic conditions; (ii) investigate varying fibre lengths and rubber particle sizes; and (iii) perform life-cycle cost and environmental impact assessments to strengthen the case for widespread policy adoption.
